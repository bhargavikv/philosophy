Thought Experiment: Monkey Island

Imagine a scenario where a group of individuals is placed in a desperate situation, like being stranded on an island with dangerous creatures flesh-eating monkeys. To survive, they would instinctively form a tight-knit community. This involves developing a common language, creating a shared story or "founding myth" about their purpose, and establishing rituals that foster deep intimacy and bonding. A crucial aspect of this bonding is the emergence of a leader willing to make the ultimate sacrifice for the group, inspiring extreme loyalty and dedication.

This process cultivates intense cohesion and synchronicity, creating a "hive mind" where individuals act as one, even sacrificing themselves without hesitation for the collective, as seen in examples like jumping on a grenade. It is theorized that groups forged in such extreme unity could, upon returning to a larger society, secretly gain and maintain significant power, passing this legacy to their descendants.




Historical Examples of Cohesion

This concept of forging extreme unity through hardship is reflected in historical military societies:
  • The Spartans subjected young boys to a brutal education system involving hazing, same-sex mentorship, and even secret killings of slaves (Helots) as a rite of passage. These practices, though disturbing, are thought to have cultivated unparalleled solidarity and military dominance.
  • The Sacred Band of Thebes, an elite unit of 300 soldier-lovers, also demonstrated how deep personal bonds could translate into formidable military prowess.
  • Macedonia's army under Philip II and Alexander the Great similarly adopted and adapted these methods to build their strength.

A Philosophical Lens on Power and Evil

To understand the deeper rationale behind these theories, philosophical concepts can be applied:
  • Kant's distinction between the "phenomena" (the world as we perceive it through our minds' filters) and the "noumena" (objective reality beyond our perception) suggests that our reality is constructed by our brains.
  • Hegel expands on this, proposing that the "noumena" is the "geist" (spirit), a universal, expanding spiritual force that shapes our perceptions and connects our minds.
  • Plato and Gnostic traditions introduce the "monad" (or "nous"), a supreme, divine source of all existence, from which all reality emanates. The purpose of life, in this view, is to return to this monad. Plato believed this return was achieved through the pursuit of knowledge.
  • Dante offered an alternative path: the monad is ultimately love, and returning to it involves the pursuit of unconditional love for others. Dante posited that the monad grants free will, even if it leads to evil, and sends messengers to guide humanity.
Within this framework, evil is understood as the denial of the spiritual world and an excessive focus on the material world. The powerful groups, in this theory, maintain their control by denying the true nature of the monad and promoting a materialistic view of reality. Transgression, in this context, allows these groups to align with specific, perhaps lower, spiritual forces within this multi-dimensional universe, thereby gaining their synchronized power.
