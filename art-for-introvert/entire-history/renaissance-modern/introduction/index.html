COPERNICUS's HELIOCENTRIC THEORY

                                We will talk about Copernicus because it is fundamentally important that you understand that science, especially at that moment, was also a tool for understanding the world and the role of humans in it. For example, the model of the universe that prevailed in Europe before Copernicus was Ptolemy's geocentric model, which stated that the Earth was the stationary center of the universe and everything else revolved around it. When it was replaced by the heliocentric model with the Sun being stationary and everything else revolving around it, it affected people's worldview and their understanding of what place they take in the universe. It affected the way they explored the world and many other things.

RENAISSANCE

The Renaissance is the very era that comes to replace the Middle Ages. I am not going to focus on it, but, of course, you know that there were no such periods in reality. The eras were constructed by historians, art historians, historians of philosophy, and so on. When we say that we move from one era to another we do not mean that it happens momentary - "boom!" and you are in the Modern Period, "boom!"" and you are back in the Renaissance. You must understand that there were no definite borders between historical periods. It is a gradual change of the worldview, of the way people contemplate things, of inner cultural meanings. What historians of philosophy, historians of ethics, historians of aesthetics, of culture and others call a transition from one period to another is actually a really long process.

                                Speaking of the Renaissance, the first thing I am going to say is that it does not begin with philosophy or philosophers, and I dare even assume that it does not begin with art, for example, with Giotto. I am almost sure that the Renaissance begins with Petrarch, with Dante, that is, with literature in the broad sense of the word, with prose and poetry. I gave two examples of poets, but still. These people - Petrarch, Dante with The Divine Comedy - they start changing the way of thinking and create the artistic world that represents completely different ways of thinking than it was before.

HOW IT SAW THE PREVIOUS ERAS ?

                                So, the Renaissance comes to replace the Middle Ages. What this means for all of us is that the system of values, the system of meanings that existed in the Middle Ages ceased to express the way the new generations of people saw the world. The Renaissance came but term "Renaissance" appeared much later and it means the revival in general. And what did it revive? Here you need to understand two aspects of this new culture. The Renaissance, or the Renaissance era, revived antiquity. Once again, antiquity became relevant, important, and popular. Remember, we had the ancient world, then there were the Middle Ages, the Dark Ages, and at the end of the previous lecture. I told you that people of the Renaissance completely dismissed the Middle Ages and the Dark Ages. In the culture of the Renaissance, these ten centuries were associated with dirty, nasty, bloody knights who did not understand a thing about beauty and aesthetics.

                                However, the Renaissance came, and it, first of all, revived antiquity with its cult of sensuality, with its philosophy, the Platonic Academy flourished again, and so on. They finally translated all of Plato's works into Latin. In other words, antiquity was revived in its various forms, but more than that, the Renaissance perceived itself as the era of cultural revitalization. Culture was revived after the collapse that it experienced during the Middle Ages. The Renaissance was the kind of cultural paradigm that valued its genius, its strength very much. It was the culture that proclaimed "we are strong, we are strong as people".

⭐️ Replacement of Value ⭐️

                                The main value was replaced from god to man. Remember, the world during the Middle Ages was theocentric, everything was revolving around the figure of God. People constantly turned to God with prayers and confessions, there was always a certain connection with the almighty. In the Renaissance, the divine still played an important role, and people still tried to comprehend it in different ways. However, the first thing that the Renaissance did was the turning the value system towards the human figure.

"The world itself and man in it was the center of this worldview"

It is fundamentally important, because in the Middle Ages, although we saw many variations, the center was still always related to God. In the Renaissance all the meanings, all the values were related to people. Here came the ancient idea that the man was the measure of all things, here came Leonardo da Vinci's Vitruvian Man, who is, in fact, the personification of man as the center and measure of everything. This turn to the Anthropos, to the human, is vital, because now the system of values became focused on the human world, not on the heavenly realm. What happened in the real world mattered the most. Therefore, thinkers, philosophers, theologians, poets, writers - they all began to comprehend our reality.

                                It became important to know how the world worked, what it was filled with. It became significant and interesting. Man became interesting as a living being. That is why the Renaissance is the era of great geographical discoveries when they prioritized exploring what Augustine called the earthly city. It is important to point out that the idea of God still existed. Everyone had a different attitude to God, but he was still present in the conversation. However, during the Renaissance God was usually viewed as something that was very far away, something inaccessible. God was considered either in everything in this world, like in pantheism, or he was removed from the world altogether, like in deism. However, he ceased to be the center of everything. That is why there was a huge turn to ethics, to social relations, and to the study of this world.

Lorenzo Valla (1407 - 1457)

                                Lorenzo Valla was a very interesting and rather provocative[5] thinker at that time. We clearly see the revival of antiquity in his philosophy, but, frankly speaking, it was quite poorly understood. This was common for the situations when current philosophers try to revive a past philosophy. They did not actually bring back the old philosophical teachings, but rather their own understanding of that philosophy. Lorenzo Valla was a Christian philosopher who tried to comprehend Christianity while adding Epicureanism to it. Epicurus invented the philosophy of hedonism, the philosophy of pleasure. But pleasure for him did not mean lying down, eating fruit and berries, doing nothing. Epicurus understood pleasure in a rather ascetic[6] sense, which Valla did not take into account. Valla revived Epicureanism in more of a secular, human sense of the word. His Epicureanism was about pure pleasure. He said

"one cannot live without pleasure, but one can live without virtue"

Here you can see, firstly, how revolutionary it was for Christian thought and secondly, how it represented pleasure. I want to remind you that in the Middle Ages art was focused on death. The main act was the act of death, because it brings you closer to the Day of the Last Judgement, to the bliss that will come after the end of time or to the lack of it. The Renaissance, to the contrary, focused on our life and viewed death as something that we should fear because after death there would be no life. And here came the priority of life, the priority of existence. Here came the art of living. As you may remember, what mattered in the Middle Ages was how you die. For example, people were all afraid to die alone. Now the way you live this life, the way you exist in this world became important. The culture of enjoying life emerged, in contrast to the culture of asceticism that we saw in the Middle Ages. People enjoyed life and made it the basis of their own philosophy. The quote

"Long live true and constant enjoyment at any age and for any sex!"

is literally the hymn of the Renaissance and we need to understand the phrase in the hedonistic sense of the words. Remember that this was the revival of antiquity. Plus, Valla was following the biblical text, although he had quite an interesting combination of antiquity and Christianity. It was typical of the entire Renaissance era: philosophers could not bring back the ancient thought in its original form, they had to transform it. People were very passionate about antiquity, unlike in the Middle Ages. These were the people who saw antiquity as the moment when people really understood the meaning of life, so they were trying to bring it back. But it could not be brought back completely, because antiquity was in the pre-Christian era, the era of ungodliness, the era of heretics[7].


Dante's Inferno

Still, thinkers of the Renaissance always tried to show their respect to the ancient thoughts and made them mainstream. You may remember that Dante put ancient philosophers in the first circle of hell because he could not just send them to hell, but he also could not send them to heaven or purgatory[8], because they were not baptized. So he put them in limbo, a place where nothing happens, so as to still have them in his Divine Comedy because his path would be incomplete without them. Besides, do not forget who led Dante through the circles of hell - it was Virgil, who was a Roman poet. Then, more importantly, the unequivocal[9] criticism of the Church and the papacy[10] began in the Renaissance era. People were trying to bring back what they believed to be the true Christianity, which throughout the entire Renaissance and the Modern Period became associated with Aurelius Augustine.

POPES AND POLITICS

                       They believed that Church Fathers truly understood the essence of faith, and they were trying to turn back to them and to go against the power of the Popes. There was this special statement, a document, circulating throughout the Middle Ages that claimed that the Byzantine emperor gave the Pope special permission to rule. This letter of Justinian served as an argument for the Pope's power being not only religious but also political. You may remember that the Popes quite actively participated in European politics. Sometimes they won, sometimes they lost. You may remember the Babylonian Captivity when the Pope was detained in France. So, the Popes had quite an extensive and diverse political life, which was what the Renaissance opposed.

                                We have been talking a lot about the context, but another important thing is that soon there would be the reformation, Martin Luther, a completely different understanding of Christianity, the search for authentic Christianity, and strong criticism of indulgences as a way of forgiving sins. Remember, the Catholic Church believes that the grace made by Christ and those drawn near is unlimited and can be shared with everyone. They sold indulgences as some piece of grace that could absolve you of your sins. Indulgences, the institution of the Pope, his unequivocal intention to rule as a temporal leader, all this irritated the thinkers of the Renaissance, and it would eventually lead to the split of Christianity into Catholicism and the Protestants. Well, Protestants were also different, if you remember there was Luther on one side, Calvin on the other, and so on. But this was all based on the philosophy of the Renaissance, on its opposition to the Pope's power, namely his temporal power. Moreover, people of the Renaissance loved the pure Latin language and Cicero. Endless re-reading and reprinting of Latin texts after Cicero turned this language into something ugly, so during the Renaissance people were trying to bring back the classical beautiful language that started it all. Overall, Lorenzo Valla marked the start of philosophers talking about the importance of enjoying life.
