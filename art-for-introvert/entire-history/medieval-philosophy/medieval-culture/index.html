MEDIEVAL CULTURE

Three pillars supported the Middle Ages: Byzantium[5], barbarians[6], and Rome.

Barbarians symbolized the medieval culture that arose from the ruins of the Roman Empire, had a bright hearth[7] of Byzantium, and was saturated with the new blood of barbarians. Remember the Migration Period: Rome collapsed and Europe acquired barbarian culture. However, it was based on the ruins of the Empire and shared a border with its larger brother, Byzantium. In the young barbarian culture, the world was understood in a more direct, ritualized, and emotional way. There was no intellectual layer between the world and a person, a human being, at the time. As a result, this was a blunt[8] culture that communicated with the world and people in a blunt manner. So, this culture was emotional, vulgar, simple, and primary, meaning that it was not concerned with transcendental issues.

TRANSCENDENT vs TRANSCENDENTAL

These are two words in philosophy that are frequently misused. For example, when we talk about Kant, we will start with this dichotomy. So, transcendent is something going beyond this world; of course, it is often something divine. That is, we have the world; God is not inside this world; God created it. The world is the result of being, God's creation. So, God is transcendent for this world, He is outside of it. Transcendental is the internal foundation of this world, whereas transcendent is the external foundation. Unlike in natural philosophy, the foundation is not something natural, not a part of nature, but intelligent, something that allows this world to exist. Kant would define it as the ability to think. There is transcendent, which is entirely external, and transcendental, which is entirely internal.

                                So, there is almost nothing transcendent for barbarian culture, nothing out of this world. Mythology is this wholesome world in which you are not separated from the world around you. So, barbarian culture is very similar to this. As a result, they appreciated bodily sensuality. So, they did not attempt to cover or conceal it. They were less interested in high matters and more in the world around them: the spirits of this world with which they could interact, rituals, rites, and so on. So, that was the culture, which appears vulgar at first glance; for example, they did not feel ashamed of making a phallus the symbol of fertility. Barbarians were not afraid to picture some erotic scenes, because they were surrounded by a sensual world. This is barbarian culture, and we can see the core of the barbarian culture in the Middle Ages.

BYZANTIUM

Byzantium was the Roman Empire's heir. First of all, it was the place where this new understanding of new medieval art appeared. It started with the abandonment of worshipping of idol statues. You remember that in antiquity there were a lot of sculptures, they were everywhere, beautiful and colored. So, because we only have copies of these sculptures, we have this image of antiquity as those white faces and white figures. The original antique sculpture had been colored, and it was essentially an idol. There is no distinction between Perun and Zeus in terms of terminology. There is no large difference between them because they are both mythological totems of Gods. Remember an ancient temple where no one was allowed inside, and there sat god, this massive statue of Zeus? So, when Christianity became the Empire's official religion, it distanced itself from Paganism[10]. on all levels, including philosophy, which we will discuss, mentality, and art.

                                Because idols were destroyed, there were no sculptures in the early stages of the development of the Christian religion. Pictorial art, frescoes[11]., and mosaics[12]. came to the fore because the Middle Ages had a major problem to solve. They needed to depict a world that does not exist physically. People and gods coexisted and interacted in Antiquity. The Middle Ages make one very important move, and the world is split in half. We have the world as the creation of God and the sacral world of God, the profane[13], and the sacral world. We will start talking about it with St. Augustine. All intentions were directed at God, at the divine.

                                So, the task was not to picture this world, which was deemed to be empty and the place of sin. So, the sensuality of the antique world was moving away. In medieval art, you won't see these masculine, full-bodied figures because the body ceased to be an important, valued part of this world. The spirit took precedence over everything else. As a result, a symbol appeared. It was an attempt to convey the divine through a physical object. That is how symbolization works. A medieval person had to imagine the divine world. Byzantium came up with the ideal solution; well, they didn't, but they began using mosaics and gold as an instrument. Mosaics and gold became symbols of the divine. Therefore, in Ravenna[14] for example, we see the apotheosis[15] . of mosaic art, which strives to show us the world that doesn't exist, the world of the divine.

                                At the same time, everything connected to antiquity, to sensuality, vanished and reappeared in the Renaissance. That is why Michelangelo depicted everyone, including children, as muscular. He resurrected the cult of the body. When the emphasis shifted to the abstract, the divine, the unimaginable, and the incomprehensible, the cult of the body, which medieval culture opposed, was neglected. All of this became secondary, even tertiary, to the most important thing: understanding the divine. The feeling of heavenly beauty is, of course, the next aspect to consider. God is the most beautiful thing in the world.

These simple ideas focus on the difference between antiquity and the Middle Ages. What exactly is antiquity? It's cosmocentrism, and the most important thing is the cosmos. Polytheism, gods who live in this world, gods representing forces of nature and dimensions of the psyche. For example, Ares is a symbol of bravery, and Athena is a symbol of wisdom, who possesses people and bestows her strength and wisdom on them. It is, of course, the body, corporeality, gymnasium, and this is the cult of the body. Mimesis, or the art of mimicking, was also very important in antiquity.

                                The Middle Ages, on the other hand, were characterized by theocentricism (the belief that God is the central aspect to existence) and monotheism. Essentially, the entire medieval philosophy adheres to it, with the exception of a few marginal heretical schools. Monotheism appears in its primary form in Europe in Zoroastrianism. Zoroastrian was the first one who said "God is one." That's where the idea of Nietzsche that God has died comes from. It was also the source of the Trinity problem, which was common to all Medieval thought. How is it possible to have one in three and three in one? What is the link between God the Father, God the Son, and God the Holy Spirit? Remember that Orthodoxy and Catholicism disagree over these issues, which stem from disagreements over God's three hypostases.

                                Then, because God exists outside of the world (God is transcendent), determining how to cognize God is a difficult task. How can we understand God if He exists outside of this world? Is it possible for a human to address God through prayer, or is God initially unidentified and it is pointless to try to find Him? The next question is how to interact with God, who exists outside of this world and is out of our perception of reality. The emergence of the phenomenon of history is also very important. It was in the Middle Ages when the idea of linear history and eschatology[16] was introduced. The history in Antiquity was primarily based on reminiscences of the past.

                                The concept of linear time first appears in the Middle Ages. There is a point of beginning ("in the beginning was the Word, and the Word was God"), then there is a certain progression of history, then Judgment Day comes, and then there is timelessness. The concept of linear time, with a beginning and the end, arose precisely from Christianity. God-Logos is also a difficult concept to grasp. With an eye to antiquity, the first philosophers of the Middle Ages used and attempted to comprehend the Logos. What does the term "Logos" mean in the Christian paradigm? God is largely transformed into the Logos. Then comes the Soul. The most important things are the soul and the spiritual path. Finally, there is symbolism, which is the art of allegory.[17] The concept of symbolism underpins all Medieval art.
